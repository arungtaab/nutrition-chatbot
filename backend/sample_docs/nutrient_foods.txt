Nutrient content of common foods and pantry staples

Knowing the nutrient profile of everyday foods helps you build balanced meals and meet goals like more protein, fibre, or specific vitamins.

Grains and starches
- Oats: fibre, B vitamins, magnesium; support satiety and heart health.
- Brown rice: fibre, manganese; more nutrients than white rice.
- Quinoa: complete protein, fibre, iron; good for vegetarian meals.
- Whole-wheat bread and pasta: fibre and B vitamins when 100% whole grain.

Proteins
- Chicken breast: lean protein, B vitamins, low fat when skinless.
- Eggs: high-quality protein, choline, vitamin D, B12.
- Lentils and chickpeas: plant protein, fibre, iron, folate; versatile and filling.
- Salmon and fatty fish: omega-3 fatty acids, protein, vitamin D; support heart and brain health.
- Greek yogurt: protein, calcium, probiotics; choose plain to limit added sugar.
- Tofu and tempeh: plant protein, calcium (if set with calcium); suitable for vegetarian and vegan diets.

Vegetables and fruits
- Leafy greens (spinach, kale): vitamins A, C, K, folate, iron; low calorie, high nutrient density.
- Broccoli and cruciferous vegetables: fibre, vitamin C, folate.
- Sweet potato: fibre, vitamin A (beta-carotene), potassium.
- Berries: fibre, vitamin C, antioxidants; lower in sugar than many fruits.
- Bananas: potassium, quick energy; useful around exercise.

Pantry staples
- Canned beans: convenient protein and fibre; rinse to reduce sodium.
- Nuts and seeds: healthy fats, protein, vitamin E, magnesium; small handfuls for portion control.
- Olive oil: monounsaturated fat; use for cooking and dressings.
- Canned tomatoes and tomato paste: lycopene, vitamin C; base for many meals.

Use this as a reference when planning meals for more protein, fibre, or specific nutrients, and when accommodating preferences like vegetarian or low-sodium eating.
